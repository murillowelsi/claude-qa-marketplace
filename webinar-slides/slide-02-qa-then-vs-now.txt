  SLIDE 2: THE QA ROLE — 2024 vs 2026

================================================================================

  TWO YEARS AGO (2024)
  --------------------
  - QA engineers wrote test cases manually from requirements
  - Test plans took days to produce from scratch
  - Regression suites were brittle and expensive to maintain
  - Bug reports required careful manual formatting
  - Risk prioritization was based on gut feeling or tribal knowledge
  - Automation engineers were a separate, specialized role
  - Story analysis was done in refinement meetings, often too late
  - Test coverage gaps were discovered AFTER bugs hit production

  IN 2026
  -------
  - AI generates first-draft test cases in seconds from acceptance criteria
  - A QA engineer can explore a live app and produce a full test plan
    in one command — with a real browser session
  - Risk scoring is automatic, ISTQB-aligned, and always documented
  - Bug reports are structured, complete, and logged to Jira instantly
  - Agents handle repetitive tasks; QA engineers focus on judgment calls
  - Story quality is flagged BEFORE development starts
  - The QA role shifted from "test executor" to "quality strategist"

  THE SPEED PROBLEM — THE ELEPHANT IN THE ROOM
  ---------------------------------------------
  Developers are also using AI.

  Claude, Copilot, Cursor, and AI pair programmers have made developers
  significantly faster at writing code. A feature that took a week to build
  in 2024 can now ship in two days.

  That sounds great. Until QA is still spending three days writing test cases
  for a feature that took two days to build.

  The bottleneck has shifted. QA is now the slowest part of the pipeline —
  not because QA engineers are slow, but because they haven't yet adopted
  the same AI leverage that developers have.

  If developers ship twice as fast and QA doesn't adapt,
  one of two things happens:
    1. Quality gates get skipped under time pressure
    2. Bugs reach production that would have been caught

  Both outcomes are unacceptable.

  THE ANSWER:
  -----------
  QA engineers need to match the developer's AI leverage.
  Not to compete — but to stay in sync.

  When a developer uses AI to write a feature in hours,
  a QA engineer should use AI to produce a test plan in minutes.

  That's what these skills are built for.

  THE SHIFT IN ONE SENTENCE:
  --------------------------
  QA engineers in 2026 direct AI agents —
  they don't fill spreadsheets.

  HOOK — BEFORE WE MOVE ON:
  -------------------------
  So if QA engineers are now directing agents...
  what exactly are those agents made of?

  What does it mean to give an agent a "skill"?
  How do teams share and reuse these capabilities at scale?
